发音重点 · Pronunciation

12 min · Key vocabulary drill · Connected speech

These 5 words from your pre-class lexicon are the building blocks of every professional introduction. We'll drill the pronunciation of each one, then practise how they flow together in connected speech. Throughout this section, you'll also encounter your prep vocabulary: greeting, impression, rapport, confident, handshake, elevator pitch, networking, colleague, background, credibility, and enthusiasm.

① Key Vocabulary Drill

Word / Phrase Phonetic Stress Drill Type

impression /ɪmˈpreʃən/ ▁▀▁ back-chaining
confident /ˈkɒnfɪdənt/ ▀▁▁ repeat ×3
rapport /ræˈpɔː/ ▁▀ whole-class
handshake /ˈhændʃeɪk/ ▀▁ chain drill
💡 Notice the stress pattern: GREET-ing (stress on first syllable) vs. im-PRES-sion (stress on second syllable). Misplacing the stress is the #1 reason learners sound unclear — even when every consonant is correct. When you build rapport in a professional setting, correct stress makes you sound confident, not hesitant.

② Connected Speech: Linking in Professional Introductions

When native speakers use a business greeting or deliver an elevator pitch, words blend together. This is 连读 (liándú). Below are 5 key patterns using your prep vocabulary — practise each one to build a natural, confident speaking rhythm.

① Consonant → Vowel Linking (C+V)

When a word ends in a consonant and the next starts with a vowel, the consonant "hops over."
"It's a pleasure to meet‿you" → /ɪts ə ˈpleʒə tə miː‿tʃuː/ ("meet" + "you" → /miːtʃuː/ — a key phrase in any greeting)
"I'm‿a marketing manager" → /aɪ‿mə ˈmɑːkɪtɪŋ ˈmænɪdʒə/ ("I'm" + "a" link — used when you introduce your role)

② Consonant Merging (Same Sound)

When two words share the same consonant, the sound is pronounced only once.
"Good‿afternoon" → /ɡʊ‿dɑːftəˈnuːn/ (the /d/ merges — single sound connects both words in a formal greeting)
"build‿credibility quickly" → /bɪl‿dˌkredəˈbɪləti ˈkwɪkli/ (/d/ merges — when you mention your background to establish credibility)

③ Intrusive Glides (/j/ /w/ /r/)

English inserts a glide consonant between two vowels to smooth the transition.
"I‿am a product manager" → /aɪ‿jæm ə ˈprɒdʌkt ˈmænɪdʒə/ (/j/ intrudes — notice this when you introduce yourself to a colleague)
"to‿impress a new client" → /tə‿wɪmˈpres ə njuː ˈklaɪənt/ (/w/ bridges "to" and "impress" — making a strong impression)

④ Weak Forms — Function Words Reduce

"a", "at", "to", "for", "of" reduce to schwa /ə/. Strong forms sound robotic.
"I've been‿with the company for five years" → /aɪv biːn‿wɪð ðə ˈkʌmpəni fə faɪv jɪəz/ ("for" → /fə/ — describing your experience to a new colleague)
"I look forward‿to working with you" → /aɪ lʊk ˈfɔːwə‿dtə ˈwɜːkɪŋ wɪð juː/ ("to" → /tə/ — classic closing to build rapport at a networking event)

💡 Tip: Start with Weak Forms (Pattern ④). When you deliver your elevator pitch, reducing "to" and "for" to /tə/ and /fə/ is the single fastest upgrade to sounding like a confident professional. It's the difference between textbook English and the English spoken at any networking event you'll attend.

🧑‍🏫 Teacher's Note: Drill each word first in isolation, then in the connected phrase. For rapport — Chinese speakers often mispronounce this as /ræˈpɔːt/ (adding a final /t/). Correct: the final "t" is silent. The word comes directly from French. Also: handshake uses the silent /d/ in rapid speech — /ˈhænʃeɪk/ is what native speakers actually produce. Listen for it.

语法重点 · Grammar

12 min · Tense control in professional introductions

A professional introduction isn't a list of facts — it's a story with a timeline. Using the right tense at the right moment makes you sound polished, confident, and in control. Below, we'll build the grammar frame using vocabulary you already know from your prep: introduce, experience, background, credibility, opportunity, networking, colleague, professional, and rapport.

Present Simple — Your Current Role

Use for your name, job title, and permanent facts. Every greeting starts here.

"I am a marketing director at GlobalTech." / "I introduce new clients to our platform every day." / "My primary role is building rapport with key accounts."

Present Perfect — Experience Connecting Past to Now

Use for experience that started in the past and continues. This is how you establish credibility.

"I've been with the company for five years." / "I've built a strong professional network across three continents." / "I've gained valuable experience leading cross-functional teams."

Present Continuous — What You're Doing Right Now

Use for current projects, temporary roles, and in-progress work that creates an opportunity for follow-up conversation.

"I'm currently working on a new product launch." / "I'm leading a team of five engineers." / "I'm looking to expand our networking reach in Southeast Asia."

Past Simple — Your Completed Background

Use for finished jobs, education, and achievements. This is your background chapter — the foundation of your credibility.

"I previously worked at a startup in Shanghai." / "I graduated from Fudan University." / "Before this, I was a colleague of Sarah's at Bain & Company."

Future Forms — What Comes Next

Use for your purpose, your goals, and what you're excited about. This closes your elevator pitch with enthusiasm.

"I'm going to present our Q3 results." / "I'll be responsible for the Asia-Pacific region." / "I'm looking forward to building strong rapport with the new team."

🧩 Grammar Frame: Present → Past → Future with Prep Vocabulary

Speaking Frame
PRESENT — who I am, what I introduce, how I build rapport:
"I'm ___. I'm a ___ at ___, where I'm responsible for ___."

PAST — my background and experience, what gives me credibility:
"My background is in ___. Previously, I ___ at ___, where I built a professional network across ___."

FUTURE — the opportunity ahead, why I'm excited:
"I see this as a great opportunity to ___. I'm looking forward to ___ with all of you."

🧑‍🏫 Teacher's Note: The most common error: students mix Present Perfect and Past Simple. Rule: if a specific past time is mentioned (in 2019, last year, when I joined), use Past Simple. If it connects to now (ever, never, since, for three years), use Present Perfect. Draw a timeline on the board with your own background as the example — it makes the rule stick.

商务会话 · Business Conversation

15 min · Real introductions · Pairs practice

Now we put everything together — pronunciation, connected speech, grammar, and vocabulary — into real business introductions. Every example below uses your prep words: greeting, professional, colleague, rapport, handshake, elevator pitch, networking, introduce, background, experience, credibility, opportunity, confident, and enthusiasm.

🎤 Teacher Demonstration — Listen and Notice

The teacher reads all three examples aloud. Listen for the Present → Past → Future structure, connected speech, and prep vocabulary.

Example 1 — Formal Industry Conference

"Good morning, everyone. My name is David Chen, and I'm a senior product manager at TechVision. I'm responsible for our professional SaaS platform serving over 200 enterprise clients. My background is in software engineering — before this role, I spent six years at Alibaba Cloud, where I gained deep experience in B2B product development. That background gives me strong credibility when working with technical clients. It's a pleasure to be here today. I'm really looking forward to networking with all of you and exploring opportunities for collaboration. Thank you."

📋 Uses: greeting · professional · background · experience · credibility · networking · opportunity

Example 2 — First Day Meeting a New Team

"Hi, everyone! I'm Sarah Lim, and it's great to finally meet you all in person. I've just joined the Business Development team, and I'll be looking after our Southeast Asia partnerships. Previously, I worked at Grab in Singapore, where I built rapport with over 50 regional partners. I learned a lot from my colleagues there about cross-cultural professional communication — different markets require different handshake styles, literally and figuratively! I'm excited to bring that experience here and to learn from each of you. I'm looking forward to working together."

📋 Uses: rapport · colleague · professional · handshake · experience · introduce (implied) · confident

Example 3 — First Client Meeting

"It's a pleasure to meet you, Ms. Nakamura. I'm James Wu, the account director here at Meridian Consulting. Let me give you a quick elevator pitch — we specialise in market entry strategy for European brands entering China, and I've personally led 14 such launches over the past eight years. My experience spans retail, luxury goods, and fintech — so I'm confident we have the right background for your project. I see this as a fantastic opportunity to build a long-term partnership. Shall we begin?"

📋 Uses: elevator pitch · experience · confident · background · opportunity · professional · rapport (implied in tone)

跨文化洞察 · Cross-Cultural Insight

10 min · Greeting customs · Adapt your approach

A great greeting in Shanghai might fall flat in São Paulo — or worse, cause offence. The same handshake that builds rapport in London might feel aggressive in Tokyo. Understanding these differences is a professional superpower. Below, we compare 5 major business cultures using vocabulary from your prep: handshake, rapport, impression, professional, confident, greeting, introduce, networking, colleague, background, and credibility.

Country Greeting Style Handshake Eye Contact Key Tip
🇬🇧 UK / US Firm handshake + direct "Good morning, I'm ___" Firm, 2–3 pumps, full palm contact Direct and sustained — signals confidence A weak handshake kills your first impression. Practise until it's natural.
🇯🇵 Japan Bow first (ojigi), then light handshake if offered Gentle, brief — let the Japanese person initiate Intermittent — sustained eye contact can feel confrontational Present business cards with both hands. Build rapport through humility, not boldness.
🇧🇷 Brazil Warm handshake + cheek kiss (beijinho) among colleagues Firm but warm, often with a touch on the arm Warm and engaged — looking away signals disinterest Physical warmth builds rapport fast. A distant professional style feels cold here.
🇩🇪 Germany Brief, firm handshake + title + surname: "Guten Tag, Herr Müller" One firm pump, then release — efficiency is valued Direct and brief — signals honesty and credibility Use titles (Dr., Prof., Herr/Frau). Your background matters — mention qualifications.
🇨🇳 China Light handshake + slight nod + "你好, 我是___" — introduce with surname first Gentle and brief — a bone-crushing grip is inappropriate Brief, respectful — avoid staring at senior colleagues Seniority matters. At networking events, introduce the most senior person first.
