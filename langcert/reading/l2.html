Section 2

语法信号词：句子的指南针

Grammar Signals — how tense, pronouns, and conjunctions guide your reading

Why Grammar Is a Reading Tool

Many students treat grammar as something you produce — for writing and speaking. But in reading, grammar is your fastest navigation system. A single pronoun tells you who did what. A contrast marker warns you the next idea will flip. A tense shift signals a time jump.

Rule: If you see a grammar signal, slow down for one second. It's telling you how to read the next part.

Signal Type 1

代词追踪 · Pronoun Tracking

Pronouns (it, they, this, these, those, which) always refer to something earlier in the text. In cloze and sentence-insertion questions, tracking pronouns backwards is often the fastest way to find the right answer.

"The study revealed significant gender gaps in STEM participation. These were most pronounced in computer science, where female enrolment stood at just 18%."

"These" refers back to "gender gaps" — not "STEM participation" or "computer science." Follow the pronoun upstream.

Signal Type 2

连词预判 · Conjunction Prediction

Conjunctions don't just connect — they predict what comes next. When you see however, you know a counter-argument is coming. When you see therefore, a conclusion follows. When you see furthermore, more evidence is added to the same point.

"Critics argued that remote work reduces productivity. However, a 2024 study of 10,000 employees found that output increased by 13% when workers shifted to hybrid schedules."

"However" tells you the second sentence directly contradicts the first. Predict the contrast before you even read it.

Signal Type 3

时态跳转 · Tense Shifts

A sudden change in tense is a signpost. Past → present signals ongoing relevance. Present → future signals prediction or implication. Present perfect → past signals a specific historical example.

信号词识别 · Quick Check

1. The policy was widely unpopular. Nevertheless, the government pushed it through.
"Nevertheless" always signals contrast — the second idea goes against what you'd expect from the first.
2. Air pollution levels in urban areas have risen sharply. Consequently, hospitals have reported a 15% increase in respiratory admissions.
"Consequently" = as a result. It signals a cause→effect relationship.
3. The company invested heavily in employee training. Furthermore, it introduced flexible working hours.
"Furthermore" = in addition. It adds another supporting point to the same argument.
Section 3

上下文推断：不查字典也能懂

Context Clues — how to decode unknown words from surrounding text

The 4 Types of Context Clues

Academic texts are full of unfamiliar vocabulary. The exam expects you to encounter words you don't know. Your job isn't to know every word — it's to use the text around it to figure out the meaning.

Clue Type 1

定义信号 · Definition Signals

The text explicitly tells you what the word means — using phrases like that is, in other words, which means, i.e., or.

"The treatment showed significant efficacy — that is, it produced the desired therapeutic effect in over 80% of patients."

"That is" gives you the definition: efficacy = producing the desired effect.

Clue Type 2

对比信号 · Contrast Signals

The text tells you what the word is NOT. If X is unlike Y, and you know Y is "generous," then X is probably "stingy." Look for unlike, whereas, while, in contrast to, rather than, instead of.

"Unlike her gregarious sister, who chatted with everyone at the party, Maria sat quietly in the corner reading a book."

"Unlike" contrasts the sisters. The sister chats with everyone → gregarious = sociable, outgoing. Maria is the opposite.

Clue Type 3

举例信号 · Example Signals

Examples clarify abstract terms. Such as, for instance, for example, including, like — the list that follows tells you what category the word belongs to.

"Anthropogenic factors — such as deforestation, industrial emissions, and urban expansion — are the primary drivers of climate change."

The examples (deforestation, emissions, urban expansion) all describe human activities → anthropogenic = caused by humans.

Clue Type 4

因果信号 · Cause/Effect Signals

If the text tells you what caused something, or what effect it had, you can infer the word's meaning from the chain of events.

"The prolonged drought led to widespread desertification, turning once-fertile farmland into barren dust."

The cause (drought) and the description (fertile→barren dust) tell you: desertification = the process of land becoming desert.

上下文推断 · Practice

Use context clues to guess the meaning of each bolded word.

1. Unlike the EPHEMERAL beauty of cherry blossoms, which fade within days, the oak tree stands for centuries.
Contrast clue: "Unlike" + "fade within days" + opposite of oak tree (centuries) → ephemeral = lasting only a short time.
2. The new policy was UNEQUIVOCAL — that is, it left no room for doubt or alternative interpretation.
Definition clue: "that is" + "no room for doubt" → unequivocal = clear and unambiguous.
3. The body's HOMEOSTATIC mechanisms — such as temperature regulation, blood sugar control, and water balance — keep internal conditions stable.
Example clue: "such as" + temperature regulation, blood sugar control, water balance + "keep internal conditions stable" → homeostatic = self-regulating to maintain stability.
4. The heavy rainfall EXACERBATED the flooding, making an already serious situation far worse.
Cause/effect clue: heavy rain → "making an already serious situation far worse" → exacerbated = made worse.

Part 1b 真题实战

Cloze Practice · 6 gap-fill questions using grammar + context strategies

How Part 1b Works

Part 1b gives you a passage with gaps. You choose the correct word for each gap from a set of options. Unlike Part 1a (synonyms), Part 1b tests grammar + collocation + context. You need all three to get it right.

Strategy: ① Read the full sentence → ② Check the grammar slot (noun? verb? adjective?) → ③ Check the meaning in context → ④ Eliminate by collocation.

1. The research team ________ the experiment three times to ensure accuracy.
Grammar: need a transitive verb (takes an object: "the experiment"). "Occurred" is intransitive. "Displayed" and "regarded" don't collocate with "experiment." "Conducted an experiment" is a standard collocation.
2. A ________ number of students are choosing to study abroad, according to recent data.
"Growing number" is a fixed collocation. "Raising" needs an object. "Extending" = making longer. "Expanding" is possible but "growing" is the most natural with "number."
3. The medication ________ side effects in approximately 5% of patients.
"Causes side effects" is the standard medical collocation. "Makes side effects" is ungrammatical. "Creates" is too deliberate. "Does" doesn't work with this noun.
4. ________ the challenges, the project was completed on schedule and within budget.
Context clue: "challenges" but project still succeeded → need a contrast signal. "Despite" + noun phrase. "Although" needs a full clause. "Because" and "Since" are cause signals (wrong logic).
5. The author ________ that climate change is the defining issue of our time.
Grammar: need a verb that can take a "that" clause. "Argues that..." ✓. "Discusses" can't take "that" + clause directly. "Speaks" and "tells" don't fit this structure.
6. The survey ________ that 72% of respondents preferred online learning.
Grammar: need a transitive verb that takes a "that" clause. "Revealed that..." ✓. "Appeared," "seemed," and "looked" are intransitive/linking verbs — they describe the subject, not what the subject did to data.

Part 2 初体验：句子配对

Sentence Insertion Preview · putting sentences back where they belong

What is Part 2?

Part 2 gives you a passage with removed sentences. Your job is to put them back in the right gaps. Each sentence fits logically into exactly one gap. This tests your understanding of paragraph structure and logical flow — the exact skills we've been building in this lesson.

Part 2 Strategy · 3-Step Method

Step 1: Read the sentence to be inserted. Identify its topic and any pronouns (they, this, these, it) — they must refer to something before the gap.

Step 2: Read the paragraph around each gap. Look at the sentences before and after the gap. The inserted sentence must bridge them.

Step 3: The inserted sentence usually either: (a) gives an example of the previous point, (b) introduces a counter-argument, or (c) explains a cause or effect. Match the logic.

Practice: Where Does This Sentence Go?

Sentence to insert: "This shift has fundamentally changed how companies recruit new talent."

Over the past decade, remote work has transitioned from a niche perk to a mainstream expectation. [GAP 1] Where once employers could only hire from within commuting distance of their offices, they can now access talent pools spanning multiple countries and time zones. [GAP 2] However, managing distributed teams presents its own challenges, from communication barriers to differences in labour law.

Where should the sentence be inserted?
"This shift" refers to "remote work transitioning from niche to mainstream" — the sentence that comes right before GAP 1. At GAP 2, the topic has moved on to "talent pools," so "this shift" would have no clear reference. Pronouns must point backwards to something specific.

Sentence to insert: "For example, a 2023 study found that students who slept 8+ hours before exams scored 15% higher on average."

Sleep plays a critical role in memory consolidation and academic performance. [GAP 1] However, surveys indicate that over 60% of university students regularly get less than 7 hours of sleep per night. [GAP 2] This chronic sleep deprivation may partly explain declining academic outcomes in tertiary education.

Where should the sentence be inserted?
The insert sentence starts with "For example" — it's providing evidence for the claim that "sleep plays a critical role in memory." This must go right after that claim, at GAP 1. At GAP 2, the text has shifted to a contrast ("However, surveys indicate..."), so inserting a supporting example there would break the logical flow.
